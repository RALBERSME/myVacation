There are some differences between the European Spanish spoken in Spain and the South American Spanish spoken in Argentina, but both versions are widely understood in both countries. European and Latin American Spanish, while generally mutually intelligible, have notable differences in pronunciation, grammar, and vocabulary. Pronunciation in European Spanish often features a "th" sound for the letters "z" and "c" before "e" or "i", whereas in Latin America, these are pronounced more like "s". Grammatically, Latin American Spanish often omits the "vosotros" form of verbs, while European Spanish retains it. Vocabulary also varies; for instance, some common objects or verbs might have different names or expressions in different regions. Pronunciation:

"z" and "c" before "e" or "i": In European Spanish, the "z" and "c" before "e" or "i" are pronounced with a "th" sound (like in "think"), while in Latin American Spanish they are pronounced with an "s" sound.

"s" sound: In Spain, the "s" sound may be aspirated (similar to a "h" sound) or even omitted in certain words or contexts, which is less common in Latin America.

"J" sound: The "J" sound is generally more guttural in Spain, whereas in Latin American Spanish, it is softer, resembling an English "h".

Elision of "s": In some parts of Latin America, especially in the Caribbean, the "s" sound at the end of words is frequently dropped.

"ll" in Argentina/Uruguay: In Argentina and Uruguay, the "ll" sound in words like "llorar" (to cry) or "lluvia" (rain) is pronounced as a "sh" sound, which is not typical in most other Spanish-speaking regions.

Grammar:
"Vosotros" vs. "Ustedes": European Spanish uses "vosotros" as the plural informal "you," while Latin American Spanish primarily uses "ustedes" for both formal and informal plural "you".
"Tú" vs. "Usted" for informal and formal "you": Both European and Latin American Spanish use "tú" for informal "you" and "usted" for formal "you".
"The" (el/la): In European Spanish, the definite article "the" has distinct forms for masculine ("el") and feminine ("la"), while in Latin American Spanish, they are often merged into one ("el/la").
Preterite vs. Imperfect: Latin American Spanish commonly uses the preterite tense to describe past actions, while European Spanish may use the imperfect tense more frequently.

Vocabulary: Regional variations: Many everyday objects or verbs have different names or expressions depending on the specific region in Latin America or Spain.
Loanwords: Some words and expressions from indigenous languages or other languages are more prevalent in certain regions of Latin America.
Colloquialisms: Regional slang and colloquialisms add to the diversity of vocabulary between Spanish-speaking areas.
